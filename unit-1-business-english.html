Grammar, Listening, Speaking, and Professional Writing

Use grammar as an operational tool: confirm facts, explain action, manage expectations, and protect guest safety.

Four Grammar and Communication Patterns

Present simple for duties and routines

Subject + base verb / verb-s

Example: The guest services officer assists passengers. Housekeeping prepares cabins. The bridge team monitors navigation.

Use it for normal responsibilities, schedules, and standard procedures.

Responsibility expressions

be responsible for + noun / -ing; report to + person

Example: The chief housekeeper is responsible for cabin standards. I report to the restaurant manager.

Use exact responsibility language instead of vague phrases such as “He does everything.”

Direct information questions

Who / What / Where / Which + auxiliary or be

Example: Who is responsible for this area? Where is the medical center? Which department handles lost property?

Ask one precise question at a time and confirm names, decks, cabin numbers, and times.

Obligation and escalation

must, must not, should, can, cannot

Example: Safety concerns must be reported immediately. Crew must not promise a solution outside their authority.

Use must for compulsory action, should for recommendations, and can/cannot for ability or permission.

Professional Phrase Bank

Introducing role

  • Good morning. I am a guest services officer on Deck 4.
  • I work in the housekeeping department and report to the assistant chief housekeeper.

Referring correctly

  • This request is handled by the shore excursions desk. I will contact them for you.
  • Please remain here while I notify the medical team.

Handover

  • For handover: Cabin 8240 reported a water leak at 0735. Engineering is attending.
  • Follow-up is required before 0900, and the duty supervisor has been informed.

Clarifying

  • May I confirm the cabin number and the guest’s name?
  • Do you require immediate assistance, or is this a routine request?

Listening Laboratory

Listen First

  1. Who is speaking, and who is the intended listener?
  2. Which exact location, time, number, or condition is stated?
  3. What action is required?
  4. Which information must be confirmed or repeated?
  5. What follow-up is expected?
Transcript: Welcome aboard MV Ocean Horizon. Guest services is located on Deck 4 beside the main atrium. The medical center is on Deck 2, and the shore excursions desk is on Deck 5. Crew members must follow the chain of command, refer requests to the responsible department, and report safety concerns immediately. During handover, state the issue, location, action taken, responsible person, and required follow-up.

Model Shift-Handover Note

0800 Handover — Cabin 8240: Guest reported no air-conditioning at 0735. No smoke, unusual smell, or medical concern reported. Engineering control notified at 0740 and technician assigned. Guest services informed and guest advised of a fifteen-minute response time. Incoming supervisor to confirm repair and contact the guest before 0900.

Speaking, Role-Play, and Writing Tasks

  1. Controlled speaking: Read the model dialogue with accurate stress, numbers, and polite tone.
  2. Information-gap role-play: Student A has the guest or operational facts; Student B must clarify, refer, confirm, and follow up.
  3. Professional writing: Produce a document using the model as a guide, but change the names, location, time, and operational details.
  4. Error correction: Rewrite the weak expressions above and explain why the improved versions are safer or more professional.
EXPANDED LANGUAGE WORKSHOP

Expanded Grammar and Communication Workshop

Simple explanation: Grammar is not being taught only for an examination. Each structure performs a work function. Students must understand the form, choose it for the correct purpose, and then add enough operational detail for another person to act.

Teacher can say: “A correct verb tense is only the beginning. Your sentence must also tell the listener what is true, how certain it is, who is responsible, and what happens next.”

Form, Meaning, and Operational Use

Pattern 1: Present simple for duties and routines

Meaning: This pattern helps the speaker organize information so the listener knows whether the message is a routine fact, a request, a condition, an obligation, a completed action, or a future commitment.

Form: Identify the subject, the controlling verb or modal, the main action, and the operational detail. Keep numbers, locations, negative forms, and time expressions close to the information they control.

Use on board: Use the pattern only when its level of certainty and authority matches the real situation. A polite form should not hide a safety instruction, and a strong modal such as must should not be used for a personal preference.

Common mistake: Students often produce a grammatically possible sentence that is operationally incomplete. Add who, where, when, and what happens next.

Professional Speaking: Build the Message in Layers

  1. Opening or attention: Use the person’s role, a polite service opening, or a direct emergency attention word.
  2. Main fact: Give the problem, request, instruction, or confirmed condition.
  3. Operational detail: Add exact person, table, seat, cabin, deck, route, count, time, symptom, or document.
  4. Action: State what has been done or what the listener must do.
  5. Closure: Confirm understanding, ownership, and update time.

Guest reassurance“Thank you for telling me. I will contact the responsible department immediately and update you as soon as I receive confirmation.”State the key words clearly; pause before numbers, locations, or deadlines.
Crew report“Cabin 8240, Deck 8 midships: water spreading from the bathroom at 0735. Housekeeping and engineering have been notified.”State the key words clearly; pause before numbers, locations, or deadlines.

Handover“The issue remains open. Engineering owns the next action, and Guest Services will update the guest by 0815.”State the key words clearly; pause before numbers, locations, or deadlines.

Listening Strategy: Listen for the Action Words

Students should not attempt to remember every word from the audio. Train them to make a five-column note:

WHOWHEREWHAT / CONDITIONACTIONTIME / FOLLOW-UP
speaker or responsible teamexact shipboard locationproblem, request, instruction, or statusrequired or completed actiontime, deadline, duration, or next update

Play the audio three times: first for the situation, second for exact details, and third for confirmation. After listening, students reconstruct one complete message from their notes.

Writing Scaffold

HEADING / PURPOSE
Date and time — exact location — person or group affected — factual condition — action already taken — person accepting responsibility — required follow-up time.
Fully developed model:
“Guest report received at 1042: strong burning smell from service cabinet, Deck 7 forward, starboard corridor. Area kept clear; no visible smoke at present. Bridge and technical control notified. Crew member remaining nearby at a safe distance pending response.”

Self-edit questions: Can the reader locate the situation? Can the reader distinguish fact from opinion? Is the correct department named? Is the next action clear? Is a time or status included?

Progressive Speaking Practice

  1. Level 1 — Repeat: Read the model sentence accurately.
  2. Level 2 — Substitute: Change the location, time, person, or item while preserving the structure.
  3. Level 3 — Clarify: A partner omits one key fact; ask a professional clarification question.
  4. Level 4 — Role-play: Respond to the full scenario without reading a script.
  5. Level 5 — Handover: Report the final status to a supervisor or next shift in thirty seconds.
