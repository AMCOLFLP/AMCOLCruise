Practice, Assessment, and Unit Completion

Complete every part. A score of at least 70% on the ten objective questions is required before the portal marks the unit complete.

Part A: Ten-Item Objective Check

1. A food allergy should be treated as:
2. Which question is essential?
3. Which message is most complete?
4. Why repeat the order?
5. “May contain” indicates:
6. What should happen if staff are uncertain about ingredients?
7. Which phrase is professional service recovery?
8. Seat numbers are important because they:
9. Absolute allergy guarantees should be avoided unless:
10. The safest immediate action in the case is:

Part B: Short-Answer Questions

  1. Explain the most important operational concept in this unit and give one cruise-ship example.
  2. Describe one language error that could cause service failure, confusion, privacy risk, or unsafe action.
  3. Compare guest-facing communication with crew operational communication for this unit.

Part C: Applied Case Response

A guest tells a waiter that she is allergic to peanuts. The waiter writes “no nuts” but does not ask about severity or cross-contact, and the order is entered under the wrong seat. The dessert garnish contains crushed peanuts, and the runner is about to serve it to the table.

Task: Write a five-step response covering facts, priority, department ownership, communication, and follow-up.

Part D: Professional Communication Task

Write an urgent stop-service message and a corrected allergy alert for the affected guest.

Part E: Self-Assessment

Teacher Answer Key and Scoring Guidance

Objective Answers

  1. 1. Safety-critical information
  2. 2. Can cross-contact cause a reaction?
  3. 3. Table 18 Seat 2 severe peanut allergy; cross-contact risk; supervisor confirmation required
  4. 4. To confirm item, modifications, and allergy details
  5. 5. Possible presence or cross-contact
  6. 6. Escalate to supervisor and galley
  7. 7. The replacement dish is expected in twelve minutes
  8. 8. Help deliver the correct special meal to the correct guest
  9. 9. The approved procedure and facility support the claim
  10. 10. Stop the dessert before service

Suggested weighting: objective check 40%, short answers 20%, applied case 20%, professional communication 20%. Require accurate cruise terminology, specific operational facts, realistic action, and clear follow-up.

COMPLETE REVIEW SUPPORT

Complete Review and Assessment Support

Use this review before attempting the objective questions, short answers, applied case, and professional communication task. The purpose is to retrieve the complete work process, not simply memorize individual answers.

One-Page Mental Map

Context: The complete dining-service sequence from reservation and seating to order taking, allergy control, galley production, service, payment, and recovery.

Main purpose: To take accurate orders, explain menu items, communicate dietary requirements safely, coordinate with the galley, and recover service failures professionally.

Central rule: In food and beverage service, accuracy is hospitality. A warm tone is important, but a friendly guess about ingredients, allergens, preparation methods, or waiting time can create serious risk.

Operational sequence

  1. Prepare the service point: Review reservations, table plan, menu changes, unavailable items, dietary notes, and assigned responsibilities before guests arrive.
  2. Welcome and identify needs: Confirm reservation or table, seating preferences when possible, accessibility needs, and any early dietary information.
  3. Take and repeat the order: Record table, seat, item, quantity, cooking level, modifications, beverage, allergy, and timing. Repeat the complete order back.
  4. Send a controlled message to the galley or bar: Use the approved order system and verbal confirmation for critical dietary information. Never depend on memory alone.
  5. Verify before service: Match the correct dish to the correct guest, check required identifiers, and confirm allergy or special-meal procedures before release.
  6. Monitor and recover: Check satisfaction, respond to temperature or quality concerns, explain replacement time, inform the supervisor, and record significant incidents.

How to Answer Each Assessment Part

Assessment PartBest Strategy
Objective checkRead all options, identify the operational function, eliminate vague or unsafe language, and select the answer that gives correct action and accountability.
Short answerAnswer in complete sentences. Include a reason, not only a definition.
Applied caseSeparate facts, risk, responsible department, immediate action, communication, and follow-up.
Professional communicationUse exact location, time, condition, action, owner, and closure or update requirement.
Self-assessmentUse evidence from your speaking, writing, listening notes, or task performance rather than guessing your level.

High-Value Model Answer Frames

  • Definition plus purpose: “________ means ________. It is important because ________.”
  • Procedure: “First, the crew member should ________. Next, ________. Finally, ________.”
  • Justification: “This response is appropriate because it protects ________ and ensures ________.”
  • Comparison: “Guest-facing language is ________, whereas safety-critical language is ________.”
  • Case response: “The immediate priority is ________. The issue should be reported to ________. The next update should include ________.”

Common Wrong-Answer Patterns

Wrong PatternHow to Correct It
“It should be safe.”Confirm through the approved chef or supervisor process and explain any remaining limitation.
Recording the allergy but not the guest’s seatLink the alert to the exact table, seat, guest, and dish.
Using “some” or “a little” when quantity mattersUse countable units: two bottles, three slices, one portion.
Blaming the kitchen in front of the guestTake ownership of the communication and investigate professionally.
Promising a replacement in five minutes without checkingConfirm the production time with the galley before updating the guest.

Final Readiness Check

  • I can distinguish allergy, dietary requirement, and preference.
  • I can take and repeat an order using table and seat information.
  • I can use count and non-count nouns accurately in service.
  • I can explain menu choices without guessing.
  • I can produce a clear allergy alert and service-recovery message.

Ready standard: A student is ready when each statement can be demonstrated with a correct spoken or written example, not merely answered “yes.”

Remediation Guide
  • Below 50%: reteach vocabulary and the operational sequence using one worked scenario.
  • 50–69%: focus on missing details, department selection, and message structure.
  • 70–84%: practise fluency, read-back, and adapting language to different audiences.
  • 85–100%: add unexpected details and require independent role-play plus written handover.
