6. Why record cabin access?
7. “The towels have been delivered” focuses on:

Part B: Short-Answer Questions

  1. Explain the most important operational concept in this unit and give one cruise-ship example.
  2. Describe one language error that could cause service failure, confusion, privacy risk, or unsafe action.
  3. Compare guest-facing communication with crew operational communication for this unit.

Part C: Applied Case Response

A guest reports water near the washbasin and later says a bracelet is missing. Housekeeping had entered for scheduled service, engineering entered for repair, and the guest’s family returned briefly during the work. The floor is wet, but the source is not confirmed. Staff must protect the area, document access, handle the missing-item report, and avoid accusations.

Task: Write a five-step response covering facts, priority, department ownership, communication, and follow-up.

Part D: Professional Communication Task

Write two documents: a maintenance defect report and a neutral lost-property incident summary. Do not accuse any person.

COMPLETE REVIEW SUPPORT

Complete Review and Assessment Support

Use this review before attempting the objective questions, short answers, applied case, and professional communication task. The purpose is to retrieve the complete work process, not simply memorize individual answers.

One-Page Mental Map

Context: The guest cabin cycle involving cleaning, privacy, linen, amenities, laundry, defect reporting, lost property, sanitation, and technical follow-up.

Main purpose: To protect guest privacy, record requests accurately, distinguish service needs from technical defects, and coordinate housekeeping with engineering and Guest Services.

Central rule: Cabin service is not only cleaning. It is a controlled process involving permission to enter, accurate cabin status, protection of belongings, sanitation standards, defect escalation, and documented follow-up.

Operational sequence

  1. Confirm the cabin and request: Repeat the cabin number, guest name when appropriate, requested item or service, desired time, and any access restriction.
  2. Check privacy and entry status: Observe Do Not Disturb indicators, follow knocking and announcement procedures, and enter only with authorization.
  3. Inspect before acting: Identify whether the matter is routine service, damage, a maintenance defect, a sanitation concern, or possible lost property.
  4. Complete or refer the task: Housekeeping handles approved service tasks; engineering handles technical repair; security or Guest Services may be needed for lost property or disputed items.
  5. Protect evidence and belongings: Do not move suspicious items unnecessarily. Record where an item was found and follow the approved custody procedure.
  6. Update and close: Record the action, status, outstanding work, responsible person, guest notification, and time for reinspection or follow-up.

How to Answer Each Assessment Part

Assessment PartBest Strategy
Objective checkRead all options, identify the operational function, eliminate vague or unsafe language, and select the answer that gives correct action and accountability.
Short answerAnswer in complete sentences. Include a reason, not only a definition.
Applied caseSeparate facts, risk, responsible department, immediate action, communication, and follow-up.
Professional communicationUse exact location, time, condition, action, owner, and closure or update requirement.
Self-assessmentUse evidence from your speaking, writing, listening notes, or task performance rather than guessing your level.

High-Value Model Answer Frames

  • Definition plus purpose: “________ means ________. It is important because ________.”
  • Procedure: “First, the crew member should ________. Next, ________. Finally, ________.”
  • Justification: “This response is appropriate because it protects ________ and ensures ________.”
  • Comparison: “Guest-facing language is ________, whereas safety-critical language is ________.”
  • Case response: “The immediate priority is ________. The issue should be reported to ________. The next update should include ________.”

Common Wrong-Answer Patterns

Wrong PatternHow to Correct It
Entering after one quick knockFollow the complete knock, announcement, waiting, and authorization procedure.
Writing “AC broken”Describe the symptom, current room temperature, control setting, and any noise or leakage.
Moving a found item to a pocket for safekeepingLeave it where safe, inform the supervisor, record the location, and use formal custody procedures.
Closing a request after sending it to engineeringKeep the case open until repair and guest communication are confirmed.
Discussing a guest’s cabin condition publiclyShare only necessary information with authorized colleagues.

Final Readiness Check

  • I can request and confirm permission before cabin entry.
  • I can describe technical symptoms rather than using vague labels.
  • I can use present continuous and passive status language accurately.
  • I can handle found or missing property without assumptions.
  • I can write a complete cabin-service and maintenance follow-up record.

Ready standard: A student is ready when each statement can be demonstrated with a correct spoken or written example, not merely answered “yes.”

Remediation Guide
  • Below 50%: reteach vocabulary and the operational sequence using one worked scenario.
  • 50–69%: focus on missing details, department selection, and message structure.
  • 70–84%: practise fluency, read-back, and adapting language to different audiences.
  • 85–100%: add unexpected details and require independent role-play plus written handover.
